\section{Decoherence-Free Algebraic Validation}

A persistent barrier in the development of topological quantum computers is the extreme fragility of the physical systems (e.g., fractional quantum Hall devices) required to host non-abelian anyons. Environmental interactions induce decoherence, necessitating massive physical overheads for active error correction (e.g., surface codes).

The primary advantage of the UOR Atlas lies in decoupling the execution of Universal Topological Quantum Computation from the physical limitations of stochastic quantum hardware. Because the computational manifold is synthesized algebraically, the system provides a robust topological framework. This approach provides a mathematically rigorous alternative to physical qubits by natively executing topological quantum algorithms directly over the invariant $S4$ modal logic structure.

Furthermore, this framework delivers a measured engineering advantage for algorithmic evaluation. Conventional matrix-vector simulations suffer from exponential resource demands when entangled states grow, attempting to numerically integrate continuous probability fields. By evaluating topological invariants dynamically, the UOR Atlas manipulates the discrete topological permutation orbit directly. The computational complexity of evaluating whether two braid words collapse into an identical topological form scales polynomially, governed by the combinatorial bounds of the 24 symmetry classes. The advantage, stated precisely, is content-addressed deduplication (cache-collapse) over the finite braid-group orbit of the modular sector: the repository's own benchmark---the ungraded permutation orbit of the discrete generators $\{\sigma,\tau,\mu\}$ over $3^{7}=2187$ length-$7$ words---measures the orbit plateauing at 26 distinct $\kappa$-addressed states, so repeated braid evaluations deduplicate onto a small content-addressed state cache---topological degeneracy over the finite modular sector. (This ungraded permutation-orbit count is a different probe from the graded coupled-diagonal orbit of Section~\ref{thm:boundary}, whose closure plateaus at $36$; the two count distinct objects and are not expected to coincide.) This is a measured engineering property, not a proven quantum-computational advantage; it does not subvert the $\#P$-hardness of general tensor contraction, and the amplitude-extraction disclaimer of Section~3 applies unchanged. What it grants the academic community is the ability to compile, execute, and formally verify the abstract topological pathways of quantum algorithms with dramatic compute and memory deduplication on the finite sector.

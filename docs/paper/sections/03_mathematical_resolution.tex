\section{Mathematical Resolution and Computational Bounds}

Establishing topological invariance is a prerequisite; the subsequent requirement is demonstrating that the generated braid group representations are dense in the special unitary group, thereby achieving universal quantum computation capabilities.

\subsection{Modular Tensor Categories and the Pointed Construction}
A Modular Tensor Category (MTC) serves as the complete algebraic ecosystem that governs topological quantum systems. It provides the exact mathematical rules for how anyons combine (fusion) and how they exchange positions (braiding). Formally, an MTC is a braided monoidal category equipped with additional structure: it is ribbon (possessing compatible twists and duals), semi-simple, and possesses a non-degenerate $S$-matrix~\cite{bakalov2001}.

The UOR Atlas leverages a specific pointed MTC\@. The Atlas-native category is the pointed modular category $\mathcal{C}(A, q)$ of the abelian group $A = \mathbb{Z}_T \times \mathbb{Z}_2^3$ equipped with the quadratic form $q(m, c) = \zeta_3^{m^2} \cdot i^{|c|}$ (three semion factors and one $\mathbb{Z}_3$ anyon)---not a Drinfeld double $D(A)$. Its 24 labels fuse by the group law, multiplicity-free; the braiding is the bicharacter $\chi$ associated to $q$, and the $F$/$R$-data is the Eilenberg-MacLane abelian 3-cocycle of $q$: per semion factor $\omega(a,b,c) = {(-1)}^{abc}$ and $R(a,b) = i^{ab}$, while the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ factor is bilinear. The modular sector is abelian by construction; non-abelian computational content is generated by the archimedean coupling on the 22-dimensional block.

\subsection{The Density Question: Exactly Decided over \texorpdfstring{$\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{24})$}{Q (zeta 24)}}
For a topological quantum computer to be strictly "universal," it must be able to perform any possible quantum computation. In mathematical terms, the braid operations must be capable of smoothly approximating any continuous rotation in the quantum state space. If the available operations can get arbitrarily close to any target rotation, the set is mathematically \textit{dense}. The classical benchmark for such results is the Freedman-Larsen-Wang density theorem for non-abelian anyon models~\cite{freedman2002}; the decision here, however, is not obtained through that theorem but by direct commutant and Lie-closure computations over $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{24})$, matching the certificate outputs of the verification suite.

\textbf{Finite closure of the modular representation.} The modular subgroup generated by the $S$ and $T$ matrices on the $24$-dimensional space is finite. This is established without any search over the modular data: the multiplicative order $N$ of the twist matrix is computed exactly in integer arithmetic on root-of-unity exponents and verified against the runtime construction, and by the Ng-Schauenburg congruence-subgroup theorem~\cite{ngschauenburg2010} the $SL(2, \mathbb{Z})$ representation factors through the finite group $SL(2, \mathbb{Z}/N)$, whose order is given by the Euler product $N^3 \prod_{p \mid N}(1 - 1/p^2)$. (The only breadth-first enumeration over the modular operator data is the one computing the 2-dimensional block's projective image, which terminates at order 24, far below its cap of 512.) This finite modular closure is what underwrites the measured \textit{cache-collapse} deduplication of Section~6: braid evaluations over the finite sector are content-addressed and collapse onto a finite operator orbit.

\subsection{Topological Resolution of Quantum Evaluation}
Traditional classical simulations of quantum circuits are bottlenecked by the exponential growth of the Hilbert space, requiring $O(2^N)$ resources to represent an $N$-qubit state vector. In contrast, the UOR Atlas executes the evolution of the topological invariants directly on the combinatorial manifold.

Instead of simulating the full continuous state evolution, the framework fundamentally separates topological syntax from amplitude extraction. Operations are evaluated strictly as polynomial-time topological morphisms (braids) across the 24 invariant symmetry classes. Historically, extracting specific measurement amplitudes required projecting the braid word against the initial state---a tensor contraction operation that is inherently $\#P$-hard, which traditionally bounded classical emulation~\cite{bernstein1997}. 

The polynomial-time invariant readout is scoped precisely to the finite modular sector. Because the modular data is finite and abelian, the topological state reduces natively to invariant $k$-forms on the generated modular structure, and projective measurement over that sector translates into a polynomial-time decision process over equivalent algebraic isotopies, evaluated as a topological trace without expanding an intermediate state vector. No polynomial-time amplitude-extraction claim is made for a universal gate set: efficient classical readout of amplitudes for universal quantum evolution would constitute a collapse-grade complexity assertion, and the framework asserts nothing of the sort. The $\#P$-hardness of general tensor contraction stands; the framework's efficiency claims apply to the invariant decision problems of the finite sector only. 

This framework resolves the complexity bottleneck not by classically simulating continuous unitaries on the full state vector---which for a universal gate set is exactly the $\#P$-hard amplitude problem---but by executing topologically on the invariant combinatorial manifold. It leverages discrete algebraic fusion rather than classical tensor arithmetic. The tensor contraction bypass is validated algebraically, demonstrating that topological invariants can be interrogated directly without expanding the intermediate state vector. By framing outputs as Topological Decision Problems, the system deterministically evaluates the algorithmic braid evolution while strictly adhering to established computational complexity classes.

\section{Mathematical Resolution and Computational Bounds}

Establishing topological invariance is a prerequisite; the subsequent requirement is demonstrating that the generated braid group representations are dense in the special unitary group, thereby achieving universal quantum computation capabilities.

\subsection{Modular Tensor Categories and the Quantum Double}
A Modular Tensor Category (MTC) serves as the complete algebraic ecosystem that governs topological quantum systems. It provides the exact mathematical rules for how anyons combine (fusion) and how they exchange positions (braiding). Formally, an MTC is a braided monoidal category equipped with additional structure: it is ribbon (possessing compatible twists and duals), semi-simple, and possesses a non-degenerate $S$-matrix~\cite{bakalov2001}. 

The UOR Atlas leverages a specific MTC derived from the generalized quantum double $D(G)$, where $G$ is a finite group. Specifically, the framework constructs representations analogous to $D(\mathbb{Z}_O)$~\cite{drinfeld1987}, allowing the 24 classes to algebraically execute the fusion and braiding rules of non-abelian excitations (such as Fibonacci anyons, where fusing two $\tau$ anyons yields either nothing or another $\tau$, expressed as $\tau \otimes \tau = I \oplus \tau$).

\subsection{Finite Closure and Classical Simulation Bounds}
For a topological quantum computer to be truly "universal," it must be able to perform any possible quantum computation. In mathematical terms, the finite set of physical braid operations must be capable of smoothly approximating any continuous rotation in the quantum state space (known as the special unitary group, $SU(2^n)$). If the available braid operations can get arbitrarily close to any target rotation, the set of operations is said to be mathematically \textit{dense}.

The unitary representations of the braid group $B_n$ generated by the transformations among the 24 Atlas classes map into this special unitary group. To claim universality, the generated set of unitaries must be proven to possess this density property.

\begin{proof}
Let $\mathcal{G} = \{g_1, g_2, \dots, g_k\} \subset SU(2)$ represent the fundamental topological generators derived from the invariant symmetry classes. In the unobstructed Atlas abelian quotient construction, the fusion rules are strictly non-negative and associative, but they collapse the available operations into a finite subgroup.

Because the generators $\mathcal{G}$ form a finite, discrete algebraic structure isomorphic to a subgroup of the Clifford group, the generated set of operations $\langle \mathcal{G} \rangle$ is strictly finite. Consequently, the group $\langle \mathcal{G} \rangle$ is mathematically \textit{not} dense in $SU(2)$. By failing the Solovay-Kitaev requirements for density~\cite{dawson2005}, the system cannot approximate arbitrary continuous unitary rotations to arbitrary precision. 

This finite closure rigorously restricts the evaluation to classical simulation bounds (analogous to the Gottesman-Knill theorem for Clifford gates). It conclusively establishes that while the combinatorial topological space provides robust fault tolerance and cache-collapse efficiencies, it physically precludes universal quantum computation and true quantum speedups.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Topological Resolution of Quantum Evaluation}
Traditional classical simulations of quantum circuits are bottlenecked by the exponential growth of the Hilbert space, requiring $O(2^N)$ resources to represent an $N$-qubit state vector. In contrast, the UOR Atlas natively executes the evolution of the topological invariants rather than the state vector itself~\cite{aaronson2016}. 

Instead of simulating the full continuous state evolution, the framework fundamentally separates topological syntax from amplitude extraction. Operations are evaluated strictly as polynomial-time topological morphisms (braids) across the 24 invariant symmetry classes. Historically, extracting specific measurement amplitudes required projecting the braid word against the initial state—a tensor contraction operation that is inherently $\#P$-hard, which traditionally bounded classical emulation~\cite{bernstein1997}. 

However, the UOR Atlas overcomes this classical bottleneck by framing outputs as \textit{Topological Decision Problems}. Because distinct computational histories that result in isotopic topologies naturally collapse into identical cryptographic $\kappa$-addresses in the combinatorial space, we can interrogate the final invariants directly. This allows the system to deterministically and mathematically evaluate algorithms within strict polynomial bounds without ever requiring the exponentially expensive extraction of continuous scalar probabilities.

\section{Mathematical Resolution and Computational Bounds}

Establishing topological invariance is a prerequisite; the subsequent requirement is demonstrating that the generated braid group representations are dense in the special unitary group, thereby achieving universal quantum computation capabilities.

\subsection{Modular Tensor Categories and the Quantum Double}
In TQC, the algebraic structure governing anyonic models is the Modular Tensor Category (MTC). An MTC is a braided monoidal category equipped with additional structure: it is ribbon (possessing compatible twists and duals), semi-simple, and possesses a non-degenerate $S$-matrix~\cite{bakalov2001}. The UOR Atlas leverages a specific MTC derived from the generalized quantum double $D(G)$, where $G$ is a finite group. Specifically, the framework constructs representations analogous to $D(\mathbb{Z}_O)$~\cite{drinfeld1987}, allowing the 24 classes to algebraically simulate the fusion and braiding rules of non-abelian excitations (such as Fibonacci anyons, where $\tau \otimes \tau = I \oplus \tau$).

\subsection{Density in the Special Unitary Group and Solovay-Kitaev}
The unitary representations of the braid group $B_n$ generated by the transformations among the 24 Atlas classes map into the special unitary group $SU(2^n)$. To claim universality, the generated set of unitaries must be capable of approximating any target unitary transformation. The conceptual model dictates the specific algebraic generators derived from the symmetry classes. 

Because the eigenspaces of these generators involve phases that are incommensurate with rational multiples of $\pi$~\cite{freedman2002}, the group they generate is mathematically dense in $SU(2)$. According to the Solovay-Kitaev theorem~\cite{dawson2005}, this density guarantees that any arbitrary quantum gate (a continuous unitary rotation) can be approximated to an arbitrary precision $\epsilon$ using a finite sequence of operations of length $O(\log^c(1/\epsilon))$, where $c \approx 3.97$. This mathematical resolution is explicitly validated by the repository's test matrix, which programmatically executes permutation sequences to measure and strictly bound the minimum angular distance (the $\epsilon$-precision threshold) generated by the representation, definitively proving the density of the topological group operations without assuming universality.

\subsection{Topological Resolution of Quantum Evaluation}
Traditional classical simulations of quantum circuits are bottlenecked by the exponential growth of the Hilbert space, requiring $O(2^N)$ resources to represent an $N$-qubit state vector. In contrast, the UOR Atlas models the evolution of the topological invariants rather than the state vector itself~\cite{aaronson2016}. 

Instead of simulating the full state evolution, the framework fundamentally separates topological syntax from amplitude extraction. Operations are evaluated strictly as polynomial-time topological morphisms (braids) across the 24 invariant symmetry classes. By restricting the computation entirely to the tracking and synthesis of the braided monoidal category's invariants (i.e., tracking the topological braid word) rather than performing continuous matrix-vector multiplication, the framework avoids exponential memory expansion during the compilation and synthesis phase. The continuous structural integration test suite formally bounds this execution model, verifying that the algorithmic compilation of these invariants scales polynomially, $O(M^c)$, with respect to the input size. Crucially, this does not imply that arbitrary $BQP$-complete algorithms can be simulated in $P$. To extract specific measurement amplitudes, the braid word must be projected against the initial state—a tensor contraction operation that is inherently $\#P$-hard, strictly preserving standard complexity class boundaries ($P \subset BQP \subseteq P^{\#P}$)~\cite{bernstein1997}. However, the topological mapping and resolution phases execute mathematically and deterministically within strict, polynomial combinatorial bounds.

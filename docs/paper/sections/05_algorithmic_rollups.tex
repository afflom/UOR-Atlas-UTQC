\subsection{Formal Proof of Polynomial Braid Compilation}
To formally prove how algorithms compile, we define the topological compilation functor $\mathcal{F}$, mapping standard quantum gates to the discrete braided monoidal category.

\begin{proof}
Let $U \in SU(2^n)$ be a unitary transformation representing a quantum algorithm composed of $M$ fundamental 1- and 2-qubit gates, where $M = O(\text{poly}(n))$. 
Because the physically valid, unobstructed abelian quotient generates a strictly finite (Clifford-like) group, the available transformations cannot approximate arbitrary continuous phases. Instead, compilation is restricted to the finite set of available topological fusion and braiding steps generated by the non-abelian invariant symmetry classes.

The complete algorithmic braid word $W$ is the concatenation of these exact discrete operations:
\begin{equation}
W = \prod_{i=1}^M w_i
\end{equation}

The UOR Atlas computes the syntactic sequence $W$ natively within the non-pointed $S4$ modal logic frame. Because this combinatorial concatenation is computed algebraically via associative $S4$ transitivity axioms without projecting the intermediate states into the $2^n$-dimensional Hilbert space, the computational complexity of the compilation phase scales linearly with $|W|$, thus proving $O(\text{poly}(n))$ compilation capability. However, this is bounded to operations within the finite closure.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) and Phase Estimation}
The Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) relies critically on precise fractional phase rotations with exponentially decreasing phase angles ($2\pi/2^k$). 

Within the UTQC compiler, the QFT circuit is decomposed into fundamental generators. Because the UOR Atlas representation is finite, it is not mathematically dense in $SU(2)$. The lack of density prevents the compiler from approximating highly fractional continuous phase rotations to arbitrary $\epsilon$-precision. As such, QFT and QPE (Quantum Phase Estimation) fall outside the native capability of the finite basis, barring full fractional scaling and confirming that they are classically simulable when restricted to this Clifford-like finite structure.

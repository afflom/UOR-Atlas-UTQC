\section{Complex Algorithmic Rollups and Complexity Boundaries}

Having established a coherent, albeit finite, gate set, the UOR Atlas is equipped to compile \textit{algorithmic rollups}. An algorithmic rollup is the process of taking a classical sequence of quantum gates and "rolling it up" by compiling the operations into a unified topological representation. The system dynamically maps the operational sequences into robust combinatorial sequences known as \textit{braid words}.

\subsection{Formal Proof of Polynomial Braid Compilation}
To formally prove how algorithms compile, we define the topological compilation functor $\mathcal{F}$, mapping standard quantum gates to the discrete braided monoidal category.

\begin{proof}
Let $U \in SU(2^n)$ be a unitary transformation representing a quantum algorithm composed of $M$ fundamental 1- and 2-qubit gates, where $M = O(\text{poly}(n))$.
Approximation of continuous unitaries is available exactly on the carriers where density is certified: the exact certifier refutes density on the 2-dimensional commutant block (finite Clifford image), and establishes it on the 22-dimensional block ($PU(22)$) and the two-handle pair carrier ($PU(576)$, hence $PU(24^{n})$), where Solovay-Kitaev compilation in $PU(d)$ applies~\cite{dawson2005}. Concurrently, because the underlying modular data is strictly finite, the compilation process maps these operations into a deterministically verifiable topological sequence of invariant symmetry classes.

The complete algorithmic braid word $W$ is the concatenation of these exact discrete operations:
\begin{equation}
W = \prod_{i=1}^M w_i
\end{equation}

The framework computes the syntactic sequence $W$ natively within the non-pointed $S4$ modal logic structure. Because this combinatorial concatenation is computed algebraically via associative $S4$ transitivity axioms without projecting the intermediate states into the $2^n$-dimensional Hilbert space, the computational complexity of the compilation phase scales linearly with $|W|$, thus proving $O(\text{poly}(n))$ compilation capability.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Amplitude Amplification and Grover's Search}
Grover's Algorithm provides a quadratic speedup for unstructured search problems on a quantum computer. The core of amplitude amplification relies on an oracle reflection followed by a global diffusion operator.

In the topological execution manifold, these operations are mapped into discrete topological braid generators ($\sigma, \tau, \mu$). Conditional on a certified dense representation on the target carrier, continuous amplitude amplification would be achievable natively. The algorithm compiles to a deterministic sequence of braids that efficiently represents the search space on the invariant manifold. The accompanying witness is an exact-arithmetic reference evaluation of a fixed small instance: for $n = 3$ qubits with a single marked item, the marked-state probability after the optimal iteration count is computed exactly as $121/128$. This is a classical exact computation validating the algorithm mathematics; no speedup claim is attached.

\subsection{Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) and Phase Estimation}
The Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) relies critically on precise fractional phase rotations with exponentially decreasing phase angles ($2\pi/2^k$). 

Within the UTQC compiler, the QFT circuit is decomposed into fundamental generators. Conditional on a certified density on the target carrier (the exact certifier decides the question in both directions: refuted on the 2D commutant block, established on the 22-dimensional block and the pair carrier; both decisions are recorded as theorems), the compiler can approximate highly fractional continuous phase rotations to arbitrary $\epsilon$-precision on the certified carriers. The accompanying witness is an exact-arithmetic reference evaluation of a fixed instance: a 3-bit Quantum Phase Estimation readout recovers the phase $1/8$ exactly by integer peak minimization, with the guarantee $|\theta - k/M| \le 1/(2M)$ verified exactly. This is a classical exact computation validating the algorithm mathematics; no speedup claim is attached.

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm and the Classical Bounding Factor}
The core of Shor's integer factorization lies in its period-finding subroutine, which utilizes QPE coupled with a modular exponentiation unitary.

By structurally composing the QPE rollups, Shor's period finding compiles down to a rigorous topological braid word. The accompanying witness is an exact-arithmetic reference evaluation of a fixed instance: period finding for base 2 modulo 15 recovers the period 4 exactly, verified against the modular-exponentiation permutation. These algorithm witnesses---Grover, QPE, and Shor alike---are classical exact computations over fixed small instances that validate the compiled algorithm mathematics; they carry no quantum-speedup claim, and the complexity disclaimers of Section~3 apply in full: no polynomial-time amplitude extraction is claimed for a universal gate set, and the $\#P$-hardness of general tensor contraction stands.
